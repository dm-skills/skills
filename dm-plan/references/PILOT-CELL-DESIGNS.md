## Pilot Cell Designs

Use this reference when the user needs practical test-cell structures for a first campaign, a format test, an audience test, or a phase-based rollout plan.

---

## Principles

A good pilot answers the biggest business question with the fewest moving parts.

Default rules:
- keep one stable control
- change one major lever at a time when the result needs to be decision-grade
- use a holdout when incremental read quality matters
- design cells so a winning result naturally suggests the next rollout step

Bad pilot design usually looks like this:
- too many cells for the budget
- multiple major variables changed inside one challenger
- no documented decision threshold
- pilot economics presented without rollout economics

---

## Standard Two-Cell Pilot

Use this when the goal is simply to prove the channel or compare a best guess against one challenger.

| Cell | Purpose | Typical Variable |
|------|---------|------------------|
| Control | Establish the baseline | Current best audience, format, and offer |
| Challenger | Test one major alternative | Offer, creative frame, audience source, or format |

Best for:
- first direct mail test
- win-back pilot
- simple acquisition proof of concept

---

## Three-Cell Pilot

Use this when the team must answer two distinct questions in one round.

| Cell | Purpose | Typical Variable |
|------|---------|------------------|
| Control | Baseline | Best current thesis |
| Challenger A | Test message or offer | Same audience and format, different commercial angle |
| Challenger B | Test audience or format | Same core message, different audience or package |

This structure works well when the team needs both:
- a creative or offer read
- an audience or format read

Do not add a third challenger unless the budget and reporting rigor can support it.

## Common Pilot Shapes

### Format Test

Hold audience and offer as steady as possible.

| Cell | Variable |
|------|----------|
| Control | Standard postcard or letter |
| Challenger | Alternative format with comparable message |

Use when the question is whether the extra production cost buys better response quality or conversion rate.

### Offer Test

Hold audience and format steady.

| Cell | Variable |
|------|----------|
| Control | Current commercial offer |
| Challenger | Stronger or differently framed offer |

Use when the team suspects the audience is viable but the value exchange is weak.

### Audience Test

Hold format and creative frame mostly steady.

| Cell | Variable |
|------|----------|
| Control | Best known audience source |
| Challenger | Alternative list, model, geography, or event trigger |

Use when scale depends on finding the right audience source rather than only improving creative.

## Phase-Based Rollout Structure

Many strong programs follow a three-phase structure:

### Phase 1: Prove

Goal:
- confirm the channel can produce an economic signal worth pursuing

Typical design:
- control plus one or two challengers
- smaller quantities
- narrower audience definition
- explicit decision thresholds

### Phase 2: Optimize

Goal:
- improve the winning audience-format-message combination

Typical design:
- keep the best cell as the new control
- challenge offer, cadence, creative, or timing
- begin testing whether economics hold across adjacent segments

### Phase 3: Scale

Goal:
- expand volume without losing the economics that made the pilot work

Typical design:
- broaden geography or audience one layer at a time
- compare pilot CPP to rollout CPP
- keep reporting cadence tight enough to catch degradation early

---

## Holdout Guidance

Use a holdout when the user needs to estimate incremental impact rather than only attributed orders.

Default guidance:
- use a holdout when the audience is large enough to tolerate it
- keep the holdout clean and stable
- define whether the holdout is permanent, rotating, or campaign-specific
- document the business cost of withholding the treatment

If the user cannot support a true holdout, say so clearly and label the readout as directional.

---

## Decision Language

Pilot design is incomplete without the decision rule.

Useful decision statements:
- "Scale only if the mailed group beats holdout with positive contribution margin."
- "Adopt the new format only if response lift offsets the higher production cost."
- "Expand into adjacent geographies only if unit economics remain within 10 to 15 percent of the pilot."
- "Keep the audience but retest the offer if response is weak and interim diagnostics show clean execution."

The goal of the pilot is not just to learn. It is to make the next decision easier and more defensible.
