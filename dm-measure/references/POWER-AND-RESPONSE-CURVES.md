## Power and Response Curves

Use this reference when the user needs practical guidance on sample size confidence, response-window maturity, or how much evidence is enough to scale.

---

## Statistical Power in Practical Terms

Power is the chance that the test can detect a real difference if one exists.

In direct mail terms, low power usually means:
- the test was too small
- the expected lift was too subtle
- the holdout was too small
- the team is trying to make a hard decision from noisy data

The important operational question is:
"Was this test designed to support the decision we are trying to make?"

---

## Practical Power Rules

Use plain language when guiding users:

- Small expected lifts need larger cells.
- Holdout-based reads usually need more scale than promo-code reads.
- If the business decision is high-cost, require more confidence.
- If the result only guides the next test, directional evidence may be enough.

Useful framing:
- directional read: enough to choose the next learning step
- decision-grade read: enough to justify rollout or budget expansion

---

## Directional vs Decision-Grade Reads

Not every test needs the same burden of proof.

Directional read:
- useful for narrowing options
- acceptable when risk is low and follow-up testing is planned
- should not be presented as final proof

Decision-grade read:
- used for scale, budget shifts, or operational commitment
- should have stronger statistical and economic support
- should usually be replicated if the rollout cost is large

---

## Response Curves

Direct mail rarely converts on a single day. Responses usually accumulate over time.

What changes the curve:
- audience type
- offer strength
- purchase cycle length
- response channel
- business model
- seasonality

Examples:
- offer-driven ecommerce programs may respond faster
- higher-consideration categories often show a longer tail
- triggered mail may show a tighter curve if the trigger is close to intent

---

## Through-Curve Read Logic

When a campaign is partially mature, report:
- how far through the expected response curve it is
- how much of the result is observed versus projected
- whether each cell is equally mature

Do not compare a 14-day cell to a 28-day cell without normalizing for maturity.

Use language like:
- "This campaign is approximately 60 percent through its expected response window."
- "The current CPA is projected, not final."
- "Early ordering favors the challenger, but the holdout read is not mature enough for a scale decision."

---

## Holdout Maturity Warnings

Holdout results are especially easy to misuse.

Watch for:
- weak incremental differences interpreted as proof of failure
- large point estimates with wide uncertainty
- cells that are still too early in the response cycle
- late-converting categories judged too early

If the read is immature, say so clearly and recommend the next checkpoint.

---

## Replication Guidance

Replication is worth recommending when:
- the first win is economically important
- the confidence band is still wide
- the audience will materially broaden at rollout
- the result depends on a new format or new data source

A second test does not need to be identical. It should preserve the core winning hypothesis while checking whether the signal holds in a nearby context.
